Building a Deadline Dashboard That Functions as a Control

Reliable grant oversight depends on ownership, state transitions, audit history, and fallback procedures—not merely reminders.

By Alissa KingJune 20, 2026 · 7 min read

A deadline is not controlled because it appears on a screen. It is controlled when responsibility, status, evidence, and escalation are reliable.

My Finance Dashboard has developed into a multipage grants-compliance application built with Streamlit and SQLAlchemy. It organizes funders, grants, reporting requirements, generated deadlines, statuses, calendar views, imports, and audit logs.

The dashboard makes workload visible. Its more important contribution is the data model underneath: each deadline belongs to a grant and requirement, has a due date and state, and can retain a history of changes. The dashboard turns red after a deadline is missed, a feature previously available from panic.

The source of the deadline matters

Grant deadlines may be specified as a fixed date or derived from a reporting period, frequency, and offset. The application can generate deadline instances from grant and requirement records.

Automation reduces repetitive entry, but it moves risk into configuration. If the reporting frequency, period end, or due-date offset is wrong, the system can generate incorrect deadlines consistently. Consistency is useful, though not always in the way intended.

Each generated date should therefore retain its rule source, effective period, and last verification. Material funder changes should trigger review of future instances rather than silently inheriting an outdated rule.

Status is a controlled transition

The project represents deadlines through states including Not Started, In Progress, Submitted, Accepted, Closed, and Overdue. These labels encode an operational workflow.

A production control should define who can make each transition and what evidence is required. “Submitted” may require a confirmation number or uploaded receipt. “Accepted” may require funder acknowledgment. “Closed” may require reconciliation of follow-up items.

Evidence over color

A green status should be supported by a recorded event, owner, timestamp, and—where material—submission evidence.

Attention needs hierarchy

The dashboard distinguishes overdue items, deadlines due within seven days, and those due within 30 days. This helps convert a large calendar into a sequence of action.

Time remaining is only one dimension of urgency. A report tied to a major reimbursement, continuation award, or compliance requirement may deserve earlier escalation. Workload, dependency, reviewer availability, and consequence should influence prioritization alongside date.

Audit history makes the control reviewable

When a user changes deadline status, the application writes an audit-log entry. That creates a basis for reviewing who changed what and when. Stronger auditability would also preserve previous and new values, evidence references, actor identity, and protected retention.

Audit history serves operations as well as assurance. Repeated late transitions, reopened items, and deadline corrections can reveal process problems that a current-state dashboard hides.

A password gate is not full access governance

The Streamlit application includes a password gate for public deployment. That is a useful protective layer for a prototype. A multiuser operational system handling grant and staff information may require individual accounts, role-based permissions, secure secret management, session controls, and logs tied to authenticated users.

Access should follow responsibility. Someone who submits a report may not be the same person who approves it, changes requirement rules, imports records, or resets data.

The manual fallback is part of the system

Databases, hosting, and integrations fail. A deadline control needs an export, recovery process, backup owner, and documented way to continue critical reporting when the application is unavailable.

The control objective

Complete and accurate obligations are assigned, prepared, reviewed, submitted, evidenced, and escalated before their consequences arrive.

That objective is broader than the dashboard, which is exactly why the dashboard can support it. The interface focuses attention; the data model preserves structure; the process supplies accountability.

Good operational software does not merely remind people that work exists. It makes the state of the work trustworthy enough to manage.
